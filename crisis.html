AEGEAN

Late 1980s - Aegean Sea

Late 1980s - Aegean Sea

Full image

Purchased by Greece in 1974, the Dassault Mirage F1CG is a fast, light interceptor with international notoriety backed up by a gleaming combat record. The cursive “Mirage” painted on the nose of each aircraft may as well be a Ferrari badge to the pilots flying them in HAF squadrons 342 and 334 Mira.

Along with aircraft France also tried to sell the Greeks a doctrine of horizontal intercepts and long-range engagements with the radar guided Mantra R.530 missile. This was a much harder sell and the HAF opted to stick to the more exciting close-range intercepts they were used to, outfitting their new Mirages with heatseeking AIM-9P sidewinders instead.

The jet in the gunsight is a THK F-16C, domestically produced by Turkey under the Peace Onyx I program and first flown in 1987. Since the Turkish invasion of Cyprus in 1974, Turkish fighter jets have violated Greek airspace almost every day, a direct challenge to Greek sovereignty in the Aegean Sea. The 6000 islands here, mostly uninhabited points of rock jutting haphazardly out of the ocean, are a barrier between Istanbul and the rest of the world and have been a thorn in Ankara’s side since the dissolution of the Ottoman Empire.

The conflict became a true point of contention in 1973 after the Greeks began drilling for oil in a disputed area of the sea. Since then it has blossomed largely off the radar as a ‘secret’ war. The HAF meet Turkish jets in dogfights almost every day but generally refrain from firing any weapons, and despite multiple crises the two countries are level headed enough to stop these developing into full blown flashpoints.

The Mirage has won this fight, but there are hundreds more to come

Full image

A VPAF MiG-17 Fresco (top left) dives on a USAF F-105 Thunderchief (bottom right), opening fire with its three cannons. The action is captured in the gunsight of a second F-105 as the American pilot lines up to fire on the Fresco.

For the North Vietnamese pilots this is their second engagement of the day, having earlier fended off four flights of F-105 strike aircraft guarded by two F-4 Phantom D MiGCAP flights - a total of around 24 fighters. The MiGs attacked over the Black River to the South of Hanoi, forcing the American Thunderchiefs to jettison their bombs and retreat before reaching the railway yards they had intended to strike.

Guided into perfect ambush positions by a network of ground controllers, the Vietnamese pilots present a significant threat to the Americans despite far smaller numbers. The VPAF engage from all directions at once, attacking head on with older MiG-17s while faster and more advanced MiG-21 Fishbeds attack from behind. Today 6 Fishbeds are flying with four to eight Frescos, one of which is severely damaged from an engagement with a Phantom earlier in the day.

The Americans return with a larger force, including an extra 'Iron Hand' flight of EF-105Fs to defend the strike aircraft along with the two MiGCAP flights. The MiGs engage over Hanoi but are overwhelmed, allowing the F-105 strike flights to break off and complete their attack on the Dai Loi railway bridge. The damaged Fresco is unlucky once more, and goes down after being raked with 20mm cannon fire from the lead Iron Hand aircraft.

MIG-23

4th January 1989 - 64km North of Tobruk, Libya

4th January 1989 - 64km North of Tobruk, Libya

Full image

At 11:55am an E-2C Hawkeye from the aircraft carrier USS Kennedy tracks two Libyan MiG-23MS Floggers taking off from Al Bumbah air base and heading North towards the carrier group. Two F-14A Tomcats from fighter squadron VF-32 "Fighting Swordsmen" are directed to intercept.

Communications intercepted by a nearby EA-6B Prowler electronic warfare aircraft reveal that the Libyan pilots have been ordered to meet the F-14s head on rather than turn away after being targeted by US radars as they usually would. As the distance between the aircraft shrinks the US pilots descend to 3000ft, flying low over the sea to exploit a blind spot in the MiG's radar discovered through Project Constant Peg test flights of clandestinely purchased Soviet aircraft.

At a separation distance of 22km, the radar intercept officer of the lead F-14 Gypsy 207 fires a radar guided AIM-7 Sparrow. The missile does not hit it's target. At 10km separation Gypsy 207 fires another AIM-7 but this also misses.

At 8km separation the aircraft are locked in a knife fight. The Tomcats break formation, splitting off to fly in different directions. Both Libyan fighters turn to face the second Tomcat Gypsy 202 head on. Gypsy 202 fires an AIM-7, downing the first Libyan MiG whilst Gypsy 207 turns to the other, firing a heat seeking AIM-9 sidewinder at a separation of 1.5km. The missile hits just behind the cockpit, downing the second Flogger.

Despite the aggressive manoeuvres flown by the Libyan pilots, neither of the MiG-23s ever activated the onboard RP-22SM radars that would allow them to target and fire on the Tomcats. In fact, communications between the ground controllers and pilots ceased after the initial order was given to intercept the F-14s. The Libyan pilots found themselves merged in a cut-throat dogfight with no authorisation to use their weapons.
